Autonomous vehicles

Self-driving cars began in the 1980s. In 1986, Navlabn of Carnegie Mellon University built one of the first computer-controlled self-driving cars. In 1995, Mercedes-Benz completed the Eureka Prometheus project, then the largest self-driving car and development project in history. The project redefines the technology for self-driving cars. Sebastian Thrun, Udacity's founder, led the Stanford team that won the DARPA Grand Challenge in 2005. The Google self-driving car project began in 2009. Now, Baidu has launched an open-source self-driving car project called Apollo. The platform helps partners integrate their vehicles and hardware systems to build complete autonomous systems. A self-driving car consists of a dedicated computer and sensors. Autonomic computing units are 10 times or faster than PCS. In the Apollo system, the computing units on the spacecraft were supported by a powerful swarm. Any self-driving car has several advanced sensors that can perform tasks such as sensing and positioning. With artificial intelligence and these sensors, the vehicle can operate independently of any human driver. The automatic driving system is divided into six levels. In level 0, the driver is the sole decision-maker of the system. The driver controls the steering wheel, throttle, brake, and all other vehicle controls. Level 1 is for driver assistance. In this class, the vehicle provides steering or acceleration support to the driver, in which the driver must remain fully engaged but can relinquish some control of the autonomous system. Level 2 is part of automation. The vehicle controls several functions automatically, such as automatic cruise control and lane-keeping, but the driver must still perform any functions handled by the autonomous system. Level 3 is for conditional automation. The car drives itself, but the driver must be ready to take over if necessary. Level 4 is highly automated, the vehicle controls all aspects of the driving experience and does not expect driver intervention. The vehicle may not have any steering wheel or any driver controls. However, in this class, the vehicle may be restricted to certain areas, such as the vehicle can operate completely autonomously within a specific geographic fence. But beyond the geographic fence, the vehicle cannot operate autonomously or at all. Level 5 is the highest level of complete automation, where the vehicle can operate autonomously anywhere a human can drive. Level 5 automation is at or above the level of human drivers in all cases. It will continue to test over the next three years to make sure it can replace all existing human-driven cars one day. Autonomous vehicles require positioning systems, the ability to predict objects on the road, and convert complex vehicle movements into lane switching sequences. As for trajectory planning, the vehicle needs the high-precision data of the map perception and prediction module to carry out the planning. For a car, the most basic control inputs are steering, acceleration, and braking to run the target trajectory, so a controller with a powerful control algorithm is indispensable. All of this needs to be tested over and over again to ensure that it will one day be able to drive accurately and safely on city roads.
